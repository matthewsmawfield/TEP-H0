The Hubble Tension—the $\sim5\sigma$ discrepancy between local Cepheid-based and CMB-inferred values of $H_0$—may partly reflect an environment-dependent systematic in the Cepheid distance scale. A statistically significant correlation is found between host galaxy velocity dispersion ($\sigma$) and the locally derived Hubble constant ($H_0$) across the SH0ES Cepheid sample ($N=29$), consistent with this hypothesis. Stratification at the sample median ($\sigma_{\rm med} \approx 90$ km/s) yields $H_0 = 67.8 \pm 1.6$ km/s/Mpc (low-$\sigma$; $N=15$) versus $72.5 \pm 2.3$ km/s/Mpc (high-$\sigma$; $N=14$), a difference of $\Delta H_0 = 4.6$ km/s/Mpc (Spearman $\rho = 0.43$, $p = 0.019$; Pearson $r = 0.43$, $p = 0.021$). This trend is interpreted within the Temporal Equivalence Principle (TEP), a scalar-tensor framework in which Cepheid periods are sensitive to the local gravitational potential. In deep potentials, scalar field activity induces period contraction that mimics diminished luminosity when interpreted through a universal period-luminosity relation, systematically underestimating distances and inflating $H_0$ values. Applying a TEP conformal correction with optimised coupling $\alpha = 0.58 \pm 0.16$ yields a unified $H_0 = 68.7 \pm 1.5$ km/s/Mpc, reducing tension with the Planck CMB value to $0.79\sigma$. Leave-one-out cross-validation confirms the coupling is stable and removes the residual environmental trend in held-out hosts. A differential HST analysis within M31 yields an inner-fainter signal consistent with density-dependent screening. These results suggest that a significant fraction of the Hubble Tension may be attributable to gravitational-environment dependence in the Cepheid distance scale.
