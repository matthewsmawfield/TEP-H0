5. Conclusion

This work investigates whether the Hubble Tension—a persistent challenge in precision cosmology—might arise from an environmental systematic in Cepheid-based distances. The key findings are:

  1. Environmental bias detected: Stratification of the SH0ES Cepheid host galaxies by directly measured velocity dispersion shows a significant correlation (Spearman $\rho = 0.43$, $p = 0.019$) between host potential depth and derived $H_0$. High-$\sigma$ hosts yield systematically inflated $H_0$ values ($72.5 \pm 2.3$ km/s/Mpc) compared to low-$\sigma$ hosts ($67.8 \pm 1.6$ km/s/Mpc).
  2. Magnitude contributes to tension: The bias $\Delta H_0 = 4.6$ km/s/Mpc between high- and low-density hosts accounts for a substantial fraction of the discrepancy between local and CMB measurements.
  3. TEP correction reduces the tension: Application of the TEP conformal correction with optimal coupling $\alpha = 0.58 \pm 0.16$ and effective calibrator $\sigma_{\rm ref} = 75$ km/s yields a unified local Hubble constant of $H_0 = 68.7 \pm 1.5$ km/s/Mpc. This result is robust under bootstrap resampling and reduces the tension with Planck to $0.79\sigma$.
  4. Internal consistency: Low-$\sigma$ hosts, which have environments similar to the calibrators, independently yield Planck-consistent $H_0$ (within $1\sigma$) without correction, consistent with TEP expectations.
  5. Anchor consistency test: Independent P-L fits to the extragalactic geometric anchors (LMC, NGC 4258, M31) yield $\alpha_{\rm anchor} = 0.029 \pm 0.023$—consistent with zero and in $3.4\sigma$ tension with the host-level coupling. This dichotomy is consistent with group halo screening: all three anchors are members of galaxy groups (Local Group for LMC and M31; Canes Venatici I for NGC 4258), embedding them in deep ambient potentials that trigger chameleon-type screening. The SN Ia hosts, selected for smooth Hubble flow, are biased toward isolated field galaxies where the TEP effect remains active.
  6. M31 screening consistency: The "Inner Fainter" signal observed in M31 is consistent with a density-gated screening transition. The inner core ($R < 1$ kpc; $N=5$) has mean density $\bar{\rho} = 2.2\,M_\odot\,\mathrm{pc}^{-3}$ with all five Cepheids above the transition threshold, providing a physically motivated screened-core control within the same galaxy.

These findings suggest that the Hubble Tension partly reflects an environmental systematic in the Cepheid distance scale. The Temporal Equivalence Principle provides a coherent framework for these correlations and generates falsifiable predictions, though independent confirmation with larger samples is needed.

If confirmed by independent analyses, these results would have implications for precision cosmology: future distance-ladder measurements may need to account for the gravitational environments of calibrator and target systems, and part of the reported local–CMB discrepancy could be attributable to environment-dependent calibration systematics. The findings presented here motivate targeted follow-up tests (homogeneous stellar-dispersion spectroscopy; TRGB stratification by $\sigma$; JWST Cepheid imaging) to more directly validate or falsify the proposed mechanism.
